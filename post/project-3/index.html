The Mission Statement:

Bellabeat, a high-tech manufacturer of health-focused products for women, wants to analyze their smart device fitness data. This could help unlock new growth opportunities for the company. The goal is to focus on one of Bellabeat’s products and analyze smart device data to gain insight into how consumers are using their smart devices. The insights and high-level recommendations discovered will then help guide marketing strategy for the company.

Part 1: Ask

Key objectives:

  1. Identify the business task:

The company better target their marketing efforts into their customer’s needs based on their usage of their fitness smart devices. With this info then make high-level recommendations for how these trends can inform Bellabeat marketing strategy.

  1. Consider key stakeholders:

The main stakeholders here are Urška Sršen, Bellabeat’s co-founder and Chief Creative Officer; Sando Mur, Mathematician and Bellabeat’s cofounder; And the rest of the Bellabeat marketing analytics team.

  1. The business task:

Given these facts, the business task is defined as searching for user patterns of usage of their smart devices in order to gain insights that would later better orientate marketing decisions. So, in one phrase it would be:

How do our users use our smart devices?

Part 2: Prepare

Key objectives:

1.Determine the credibility of the data:

The data is public data from FitBit Fitness Tracker Data (CC0: Public Domain, dataset made available through Mobius): This Kaggle data set contains personal fitness tracker from thirty FitBit users. Thirty eligible Fitbit users consented to the submission of personal tracker data, including minute-level output for physical activity, heart rate, and sleep monitoring. It includes information about daily activity, steps, and heart rate that can be used to explore users’ habits. This database is somewhat credible and has many different aspects on the user behavior of the trackers. However, it has a low number of subjects, is from a third party provider (Amazon Merchanical Turk) with little background information.

Based on this scatterplot, the trendline initially shows there isn’t any correlation between calories burnt and amount of sleep, but on further analysis in the next graph using a box plot, we can see …

ggplot(data = daily_activity_sleep) +
  geom_boxplot(mapping = aes(x=step_categories, y=Calories, fill=step_categories))

… there seems to be a clear relation between sleep and calories burnt.

This is an interesting chart, which shows a few unique observations -

  • People walking less than 5km a day burn between 1400 - 2200 calories. Similarly, people walking between 5km - 10km burn between 2000 - 3200 calories and people walking more than 10km a day burn between 2800 - 4000 calories a day

  • People who tend to sleep less than 6 hours a day (undersleeping) and people who tend to sleep more than 8 hours a day (oversleeping) burn fewer calories while walking similar number of steps and covering a similar distance in comparison to people sleeping 6-8 hours a day (optimal sleep)

  • The area analysis for each graph also shows the the variance of calories burnt for a persons oversleeping or undersleeping is more wide compared to those who sleep optimally.

Step count also shows that people who sleep the optimal time will on average have more steps and be more active. I like this chart a lot, because it shows very clearly the relation between the step activity level and sleep quality as the optimal range for sleeping shows an increase in the average amount of steps.

  • People who get the optimal sleep (Between 6-8 hours) have a higher overall average total steps than people who do not get enough sleep (less than 6 hours) and people who may oversleep (more than 8 hours).
  • This shows step count is another important measure for burning calories.

Recommendations

Based on the analysis conducted above, the following are my recommendations to Urška Sršen and the marketing team:

  • The graphs above show there is a relationship between sleep and calories burnt. This observation can show the customers the benefits of tracking sleep in achieving their weight loss goals.

  • The amount of steps is another important measure for calories burnt. Thus there are benefits of logging exercise data in a customers weight loss journey.

  • Tracking sleep can help regularize sleeping patterns, which leads to a better quality of life. This is a position the marketing team can explore. The data also shows a clear trend of better quality sleep linked to the activity level that would be interesting to improve your sleep and in general to low stress and improving your quality of life.

With all this in mind I would suggest focusing the marketing messages on improving sleep and on the fitness aspects of the devices. Using this device to track your sleep and activity will encourage the user to increase their activities seeing the improvement in their metrics.
